Why Most Nigerian Business Websites Don't Convert — And What to Do About It

A slow, untrustworthy website isn't neutral. It actively costs you clients. After building websites for businesses across Nigeria and Ireland, here's what we see most often — and how to fix it.

The problem most owners don't see

Most business owners judge their website the same way they judge their office — by how it looks to them. But your website isn't for you. It's for the person who just heard your name for the first time and opened their phone to check if you're real.

That person makes a decision in about 3 seconds. If your site takes 6 seconds to load, they've already left. If it looks like it was built in 2014, they've already decided you're not serious. If they can't tell what you do in 5 seconds of reading, you've lost them.

What we see most often

These are the patterns we encounter repeatedly when clients come to us after trying cheaper solutions:

  • Slow loading on mobile networks. Most Nigerian users are on mobile data, often on 4G. A website optimised for fibre broadband will feel broken to them. Large uncompressed images are usually the culprit.
  • No clear call to action. The website shows what the business does, but never tells the visitor what to do next. No phone number prominent on the page. No "book a call" button. No WhatsApp link. The visitor has to work to contact you.
  • Template sites with no real identity. When you use a generic template, your website looks identical to 200 other businesses. There is no reason for a visitor to trust you over anyone else.
  • Contact forms that go nowhere. We've seen businesses lose weeks of leads because their contact form was broken and they didn't know. Submissions were going to a spam folder or a disconnected email.
  • No social proof. Nigerians research before they buy. If a visitor can't find a single reference to your real clients or results, they don't move forward.

A useful test: open your website on your phone, on mobile data, at 9am. Time how long it takes to load. Then find your phone number. If either of those took more than 3 seconds, you have a problem.

What a conversion-focused website actually needs

This isn't about design trends or colour palettes. It's about reducing the distance between "I found this business" and "I contacted them."

  • Load in under 3 seconds on mobile data. This is non-negotiable in 2026.
  • Clear headline above the fold. A visitor should know exactly what you do before they scroll. Not a slogan. A clear sentence.
  • One dominant call to action. WhatsApp works better than email for most Nigerian businesses. Put it where it's impossible to miss.
  • Real evidence. Photos of your work, names of clients who've agreed to be mentioned, specific results if you have them.
  • Works on every screen. If your website breaks on an older Android, you're losing a significant portion of your audience.

The business case

A well-built website is not a cost. It's the best salesperson you have — one that works 24 hours a day, never calls in sick, and reaches people you'll never meet in person.

Most of the businesses we work with see a meaningful increase in enquiries within the first month of launching a properly built site. Not because we did anything magical. Because the previous site was actively sending people away.

If your current website is costing you clients, the cost of fixing it is much smaller than the cost of keeping it broken.
